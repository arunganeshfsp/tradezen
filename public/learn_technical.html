Price Action Fundamentals

⚡ 30 XP

Price action is the study of a security's price movement over time — without relying on lagging indicators. It is the purest form of market analysis. Every indicator you'll ever use is derived from price; learning to read price directly makes you a better trader regardless of which tools you use.

A candlestick tells you four things in 5 minutes: where price opened, where it went high, where it went low, and where it closed. That one candle contains more information than most people realise.

The Two Forces: Buyers vs Sellers

Every candlestick is the result of a battle. The candle body shows who won. The wicks show where the other side fought back.

▲
Bullish (Green) Candle

Close is higher than open. Buyers won the session. A large green body with small wicks means buyers dominated with little resistance.

▼
Bearish (Red) Candle

Close is lower than open. Sellers dominated. A long red body with tiny wicks = a clean bearish session with no meaningful buyer response.

—
Doji (Indecision)

Open ≈ Close. Neither side won. The market is paused. At a key level, a Doji is a powerful signal — the winning trend may be about to stall.

Reading Wicks

Wicks are rejected price levels. They tell you where the market tried to go but couldn't sustain.

💡

Long upper wick = sellers pushed price back from the high. Bearish rejection. If this happens at a resistance level, it's a strong sell signal.

Long lower wick = buyers stepped in from the low. Bullish rejection. If this happens at a support level, it's a strong buy signal (Hammer pattern).

Nifty Context

On a 5-minute Nifty chart, each candle covers 5 minutes of actual trading. Opening session candles (9:15–9:30 AM) are often the most volatile and set the tone for the day. Never trade the first 2–3 candles blindly — wait for the market to show direction before entering.

🇮🇳

Indian market tip: Nifty opens at 9:15 AM IST. The first 15 minutes form the Opening Range. Most institutional decisions are made after 9:30 AM. Retail traders who wait for the first 15-minute candle to close before acting avoid the majority of false signals.

Candlestick Patterns

⚡ 40 XP

Candlestick patterns are formations of 1–3 candles that signal a probable shift or continuation in price direction. They originated in Japanese rice markets in the 18th century and remain some of the most reliable visual signals in modern markets.

Patterns only have meaning in context — a bullish reversal pattern only matters if it appears at a support level or after a downtrend. A pattern in the middle of nowhere is noise.

Single Candle Patterns

Hammer
BULLISH REVERSAL
Long lower wick (≥2× body), small body at top. Appears at support — buyers aggressively rejected the lows.
Shooting Star
BEARISH REVERSAL
Long upper wick (≥2× body), small body at bottom. At resistance — sellers rejected the highs sharply.
Doji
INDECISION
Open ≈ Close. Neither buyers nor sellers won. At key levels, signals trend exhaustion.
Bullish Marubozu
STRONG BULL
Full green body, no wicks. Pure buying. Trend continuation signal — buyers in complete control.
Bearish Marubozu
STRONG BEAR
Full red body, no wicks. Pure selling. Strong continuation or reversal signal when breaking support.
Inverted Hammer
POTENTIAL REVERSAL
Long upper wick after downtrend. Buyers tried to push up. Needs next candle confirmation — not standalone.

Two-Candle Patterns

↑
Bullish Engulfing

A small red candle followed by a large green candle whose body completely engulfs the red body. Strong reversal signal at support. The second candle proves buyers overwhelmed the previous sellers.

↓
Bearish Engulfing

A small green candle followed by a large red candle that engulfs it. At resistance, signals a shift to selling. Very reliable when combined with overbought RSI.

⟂
Harami

A large candle followed by a small candle whose body fits entirely inside the first. The large momentum has paused — the market is hesitating. Watch for confirmation in the next candle.

Three-Candle Patterns

⭐
Morning Star

Red candle → Small body (star) → Green candle. Classic bottom reversal. The star shows sellers losing momentum. The final green candle must close above the midpoint of the first red candle for confirmation.

⭐
Evening Star

Green candle → Small body (star) → Red candle. Classic top reversal. Mirror of Morning Star. At resistance, this pattern consistently signals a change in trend on all timeframes.

📌

Rule of thumb: Single candle patterns need context. Two-candle patterns need confirmation. Three-candle patterns are largely self-confirming. Always ask: "Where did this pattern form?" A Hammer at CPR BC is far more powerful than a Hammer in the middle of a range.

Support & Resistance

⚡ 35 XP

Support is a price level where demand is strong enough to prevent further decline. Resistance is where supply overwhelms demand and prevents further rise. These are the most fundamental concepts in all of technical analysis.

S/R levels form because of price memory — traders remember where price reversed before, and they act at those levels again. Institutional participants often place large orders at round numbers and prior highs/lows, creating self-fulfilling levels.

How to Identify Strong S/R Levels

1
Multiple Touches

A level that has been tested and held 3+ times is far more significant than one that's held only once. Each touch that holds the level strengthens it.

2
Prior Swing Highs/Lows

The most important levels on any chart are prior swing highs (old resistance) and swing lows (old support). These are where institutional orders reside.

3
Round Numbers (Psychological Levels)

Nifty 18000, 19000, 20000, 21000 — these are major psychological magnets. The market gravitates toward and reacts sharply at these numbers because traders and algorithms alike cluster orders there.

4
High Volume Areas

Levels where abnormally high volume traded in the past. Heavy volume means many participants took positions there — they'll defend or exit at those levels again.

Role Reversal — The Most Powerful Concept

When a support level breaks, it becomes new resistance. When resistance breaks, it becomes new support. This role reversal is one of the most tradeable setups in the market.

🔄

Classic Nifty example: Nifty held 19,200 as support for 3 weeks. When it broke below with volume, 19,200 immediately became resistance on every rally back up. Traders who bought the retest of 19,200 from below (as resistance) got one of the cleanest short entries of the year.

False Breakouts (Bull Traps / Bear Traps)

A false breakout occurs when price pierces a level, triggers stop orders, then reverses sharply. These are deliberately engineered by large players to accumulate or distribute positions.

⚠️

How to avoid traps: Wait for a candle to close beyond the level — not just touch it. A wick through resistance with a close below is not a breakout. A strong body close above resistance with above-average volume is a real breakout.

ScenarioWhat it MeansAction
Price bounces at supportBuyers defending the levelBuy near support, SL below
Price rejects at resistanceSellers absorbing buyingSell near resistance, SL above
Resistance breaks with volumeLevel flipped — now supportBuy the retest from above
Support breaks with volumeLevel flipped — now resistanceSell the retest from below

Volume Analysis

⚡ 30 XP

Volume is the fuel of price movement. A breakout on high volume is real. A breakout on low volume is suspect. Volume tells you the conviction behind every price move.

✓
High Volume Breakout

When price breaks a key level on volume 1.5–3× the average, the move has institutional participation. These moves tend to follow through. Enter on the first pullback.

✗
Low Volume Breakout

Breakout on <0.8× average volume = weak signal. Large players haven't committed. These often fail — wait for re-test or avoid entirely.

!
Volume Climax

Extremely high volume spike at the end of a trend (exhaustion volume). This is when the last buyers/sellers get trapped. Often marks a major reversal point — the exact opposite of what most retail traders think.

↗
Volume Divergence

Price makes new highs but volume is declining — bullish momentum is weakening. This is bearish divergence. Conversely, price making new lows with declining volume is bullish (sellers losing steam).

🇮🇳

Nifty F&O expiry tip: On weekly expiry Thursdays, volume spikes dramatically due to options unwinding. Price moves on expiry day often don't follow standard S/R rules — treat them with extra caution and reduce position size.

CPR — Central Pivot Range

⚡ 45 XP

CPR is a set of three levels — TC (Top Central), PP (Pivot), BC (Bottom Central) — calculated from the previous day's High, Low, and Close. It tells you the market's likely bias before the opening bell.

TC
Top of CPR zone
PP
Pivot point
BC
Bottom of CPR zone
CPR WidthDay TypeStrategy
Narrow (<40 pts)Strong trend expectedPick a direction after ORB, ride it
Moderate (40–80)Mixed, wait and watchWait for confirmed breakout first
Wide (>80 pts)Range/choppy dayMean reversion — buy BC, sell TC

VWAP in Practice

⚡ 35 XP

VWAP (Volume Weighted Average Price) is the average price at which an instrument has traded throughout the day, weighted by volume. It is the most widely used intraday benchmark by institutional traders globally.

Every large fund, desk, and algorithm uses VWAP as a reference. When price is above VWAP, institutions consider it expensive — they prefer to sell or avoid buying. When below VWAP, they consider it cheap — prefer to buy.

↑
Price Above VWAP

Bullish intraday structure. VWAP acts as dynamic support. In a strong bull day, price will bounce off VWAP multiple times. Trade long, use VWAP dips as entries.

↓
Price Below VWAP

Bearish intraday structure. VWAP becomes dynamic resistance. Rallies to VWAP fail. Trade short, use VWAP retests as entries.

⟺
Price Oscillating Around VWAP

Range day — no directional bias. Both sides fighting. Avoid trend trades; use VWAP as the mean to fade extreme moves away from it.

⚡

Key rule: VWAP resets every day at market open (9:15 AM). It's an intraday-only tool — it has no meaning on daily or weekly charts. Never use VWAP as a positional trade signal.

EMA Strategies

⚡ 35 XP

The Exponential Moving Average (EMA) gives more weight to recent prices than a simple moving average, making it more responsive to current market conditions. The key EMAs for intraday Nifty trading are EMA 9, 21, 50, and 200.

🚀
EMA 9/21 Crossover
When EMA 9 crosses above EMA 21 on the 15-min chart, trend turns bullish. Below = bearish. Primary intraday signal used by most algo systems.
Buy: EMA 9 crosses above EMA 21
Sell: EMA 9 crosses below EMA 21
SL: Below EMA 21
📏
EMA as Dynamic S/R
In a strong uptrend, EMA 21 acts as moving support. Each dip to EMA 21 is a buy opportunity. In downtrend, EMA 21 is resistance.
Setup: Strong trend in place
Entry: First touch of EMA 21
SL: Close below EMA 21
⚡
Golden/Death Cross
EMA 50 crossing above EMA 200 on the daily chart = Golden Cross (major bull signal). Below = Death Cross. Used for positional trade bias.
Golden Cross: EMA50 > EMA200
Death Cross: EMA50 < EMA200
Timeframe: Daily chart

RSI — Reading Momentum

⚡ 30 XP

The Relative Strength Index (RSI) measures the speed and magnitude of price changes on a scale of 0–100. Above 70 = overbought. Below 30 = oversold. Developed by J. Welles Wilder, it's one of the most reliable momentum oscillators.

RSI ReadingMarket StateInterpretation
> 70OverboughtMomentum is extreme — watch for reversal. Don't short just because RSI is high in a strong trend.
50–70Bullish zonePrice is in an uptrend. Dips toward 50 are buy opportunities in a bull trend.
30–50Bearish zonePrice is in a downtrend. Rallies toward 50 are sell opportunities in a bear trend.
< 30OversoldMomentum is extreme to downside — watch for bounce. Don't buy just because RSI is low in a strong downtrend.

RSI Divergence — The Hidden Signal

↑
Bearish Divergence

Price makes a higher high but RSI makes a lower high. Momentum is weakening even as price rises. Strong warning that the uptrend is exhausting — look to reduce longs or prepare for short.

↑
Bullish Divergence

Price makes a lower low but RSI makes a higher low. Selling momentum is fading even as price declines. Often precedes a strong bounce — particularly powerful at major support levels.

Options Chain Basics

⚡ 50 XP

The options chain is a real-time snapshot of all available call and put contracts for a given underlying. Reading it correctly tells you where smart money is positioned and where significant levels lie.

CE
Call Options (CE)

The right to buy Nifty at a strike price. High Call OI at a strike = resistance level. Writers (sellers) of calls are paid to keep Nifty below that strike — they defend it aggressively.

PE
Put Options (PE)

The right to sell Nifty at a strike price. High Put OI at a strike = support level. Put writers defend Nifty above that strike. Where puts are heaviest = the floor the market is trying to protect.

PCR
Put-Call Ratio

PCR = Total Put OI ÷ Total Call OI. PCR > 1.2 = bullish (more puts written, sellers betting market won't fall). PCR < 0.8 = bearish. PCR 0.8–1.2 = neutral.

⚡
Max Pain

The strike price where the maximum number of options (both CE and PE) expire worthless — causing maximum loss to option buyers. Near expiry, Nifty gravitates toward max pain. Used as a target for expiry-day trades.

Psychology & Risk Management

⚡ 40 XP

Trading psychology and risk management separate profitable traders from everyone else. You can master every indicator and pattern in this course — none of it matters if you can't control your emotions and protect your capital.

The 2% Rule

Never risk more than 2% of your total trading capital on a single trade. If your capital is ₹5,00,000, your maximum loss per trade is ₹10,000. This rule alone keeps you in the game long enough to become consistently profitable.

⚠️

Position sizing formula:
Units to trade = (Capital × Risk%) ÷ (Entry − Stop Loss)
Example: ₹5L × 2% = ₹10,000 risk. If SL is 50 pts away on Nifty, trade 200 units (₹10,000 ÷ ₹50).

The Golden Rules

1
Always define your stop loss before entering

If you don't know where you're wrong before you enter, you have no plan. Stop losses are not optional — they are the entire foundation of risk management.

2
Minimum 1:2 Risk-Reward Ratio

Risk ₹1 to make ₹2. With a 1:2 RR, you can be wrong 40% of the time and still be profitable. Most retail traders lose because they have 1:1 or worse RR.

3
Never average a losing trade

Adding to a losing position (averaging down) is the fastest way to blow an account. The market is telling you you're wrong. Listen to it.

4
Follow your plan, not your emotions

Write your plan before market open — what you'll buy, where you'll enter, where you'll exit. Stick to it. Decisions made during a live trade are made under adrenaline, not logic.

5
Stop after 2 consecutive losses

Two losses in a row means either the market conditions have changed or you're in the wrong mindset. Walk away, reset. Tomorrow is a new day with new opportunities.
