Candlestick Patterns

Individual candles tell you what happened in one session. Candlestick patterns tell you what is about to happen next. Learn the 12 most important single, two-candle, and three-candle patterns that appear repeatedly in Nifty and BankNifty — with real price levels to anchor every concept.

Why Patterns Work

Candlestick patterns were developed by Japanese rice traders in the 18th century. They survived for 300 years because they capture something fundamental: the psychology of buyers and sellers at key price levels. When a Hammer forms at support, it is not magic — it represents a real-world event: sellers drove price down hard, buyers came in and overwhelmed them, and the session closed near the open. That's a story about who won control.

Modern institutional traders still pay attention to these patterns — not because they believe in them religiously, but because enough participants watch them to create self-fulfilling reactions at major levels. When Nifty prints a Bullish Engulfing at a 200-day EMA, thousands of traders simultaneously see it on their charts and buy. That buying itself becomes the catalyst.

📌

The Rule of Context: A pattern in isolation is noise. A pattern at a key level (support, resistance, CPR, VWAP, prior high/low) is a signal. This is the single most important concept in this entire chapter.

Single vs Two-Candle vs Three-Candle Patterns

Patterns vary in the number of candles required to form. Each type has a different reliability profile:

TypeCandlesSpeedReliabilityExamples
Single candle1Fastest signalModerate — needs contextHammer, Doji, Marubozu
Two-candle2One-day lagGood — confirmation built inEngulfing, Harami
Three-candle3Two-day lagHighest — multi-session agreementMorning Star, Evening Star

In intraday Nifty trading on a 5-minute or 15-minute chart, "candles" can be individual bars within the session. The same pattern logic applies — just compressed into minutes instead of days.

🎯

Practical filter: On intraday charts, focus on patterns that form at CPR levels, ORB (Opening Range) high/low, prior day high/low, and VWAP. On daily charts, focus on patterns at 20 EMA, 200 EMA, and weekly support/resistance.

2

The Hammer & Inverted Hammer

The Hammer is arguably the most recognizable bullish reversal pattern. It signals that sellers tried aggressively to drive price lower during the session but were completely rejected — buyers flooded in near the lows and pushed price back up to close near the open.

Hammer Structure

HAMMER vs INVERTED HAMMER vs SHOOTING STAR — VISUAL COMPARISON
HAMMERBullish reversalLower wick ≥ 2× body
HAMMER (bear)Still bullish reversalColor less important
INVERTED HAMMERBullish reversalUpper wick ≥ 2× body
SHOOTING STARBearish reversalAt top of uptrend

Hammer Rules

1
Small real body at the top

The open and close should be within 30% of the high. The body can be green or red — colour matters less than structure.

2
Lower wick at least 2× the body length

The longer the lower wick, the stronger the rejection. A wick that is 3–4× the body is extremely bullish.

3
Must appear after a downtrend or at support

A Hammer forming at a new high means nothing. Context — a drop of several candles or a key support zone — is mandatory.

4
Confirmation: next candle should close higher

Aggressive traders enter at the close of the Hammer. Conservative traders wait for the next candle to open and hold above the Hammer's close.

Nifty Example — 15-min chart · Jan 2024
Nifty had been falling for three 15-min candles from 21,820 to 21,712. The fourth candle opened at 21,715, dropped to a low of 21,668, then recovered sharply to close at 21,708. Body = 7 pts, Lower wick = 47 pts — that is a 6.7× ratio. This Hammer formed directly on the CPR Bottom Pivot at 21,670. The next candle opened gap-up at 21,730 and ran to 21,810 within two candles. The entry for a long was the next candle's open at 21,730 with a stop below the Hammer low at 21,668.

Inverted Hammer — The Overlooked Cousin

The Inverted Hammer looks like an upside-down Hammer: small body at the bottom, long upper wick, tiny or no lower wick. It appears at the bottom of a downtrend and is also a bullish reversal signal. The long upper wick shows that buyers pushed price aggressively higher — but couldn't hold it. Even though sellers partially won that session, the buyers' attempt shows intent. The next session's direction confirms or denies.

✅

Reliability boost: A green (bullish) Hammer is marginally more reliable than a red one because it shows buyers completely took over. Either colour can be traded, but green body = extra conviction for the trade.

3

Shooting Star & Hanging Man

These two patterns are the bearish mirror images of the Inverted Hammer and Hammer respectively. The structure is identical — only the context (location in trend) changes their meaning.

PatternStructureLocationSignal
Shooting StarLong upper wick, small body at bottomTop of uptrend / at resistanceBearish reversal
Hanging ManLong lower wick, small body at topTop of uptrend / at resistanceBearish reversal warning
Inverted HammerLong upper wick, small body at bottomBottom of downtrend / at supportBullish reversal
HammerLong lower wick, small body at topBottom of downtrend / at supportBullish reversal

Notice the pattern: same shape, different location = opposite meaning. This is why context is the primary filter, not the pattern shape alone.

Nifty Example — Shooting Star · Mar 2024 Daily Chart
Nifty had been in a strong uptrend, climbing from 21,000 to a high of 22,526 over 8 sessions. On March 14, the daily candle opened at 22,400, spiked to 22,526 (new all-time high at the time), then sold off hard to close at 22,302. Body = 98 pts, Upper wick = 224 pts. Classic Shooting Star structure at a key resistance zone. Over the next 4 days, Nifty retraced to 21,884. Stop for short traders: above the Shooting Star high at 22,526.

Shooting Star vs Inverted Hammer — How to Tell Them Apart

⚠️

These two patterns look identical in isolation. The only difference is trend context. Always scroll left before naming a pattern — if the candle appears after a sustained move UP, it is a Shooting Star (bearish). If it appears after a sustained move DOWN, it is an Inverted Hammer (bullish).

4

Doji Varieties

A Doji forms when the open and close are nearly equal — the real body is extremely thin or invisible. It represents a battle between buyers and sellers that ended in a draw. After a strong directional move, a Doji signals exhaustion — neither side could push further. It is one of the most important signals for market turning points.

Four Doji Types

DOJI FAMILY — ALL SIGNAL INDECISION BUT WITH DIFFERENT NUANCE
STANDARD DOJIPure indecisionEqual upper/lower wicks
LONG-LEGGED DOJIHigh volatility indecisionBoth sides fought hard
GRAVESTONE DOJIBearish at topBuyers pushed up, failed
DRAGONFLY DOJIBullish at bottomSellers pushed down, failed

The Gravestone Doji is particularly powerful at resistance: it shows buyers initially took control (long upper wick) but were completely beaten back to the open by the close. It is the strongest single-candle bearish reversal signal. The Dragonfly Doji does the opposite at support.

Nifty Example — Gravestone Doji at All-Time High · Sept 2024
Nifty had rallied to 25,670 on Sept 26, a fresh all-time high. The daily candle: Open 25,590, High 25,670, Low 25,561, Close 25,601. Body = 11 pts (near-Doji). Upper wick = 69 pts, lower wick = 29 pts. This near-Gravestone Doji at ATH preceded a 7% correction over the next 3 weeks down to 23,893. The long upper wick showed strong seller presence at the new high — a warning to long holders to tighten stops.
💡

Doji in Nifty intraday: Look for Dojis on the 15-min chart between 10:30 AM and 12:30 PM after a directional morning session. A Doji at VWAP during this period often signals a mid-session reversal. The standard Doji is most common; Gravestone/Dragonfly Dojis are rarer but more powerful.

Marubozu — The Power Candle

The Marubozu (Japanese for "close-cropped" or "bald") has no wicks — or negligible wicks. It means the market opened at one extreme and closed at the other without any hesitation. It is the purest expression of directional conviction in a candlestick.

MARUBOZU — FULL CONVICTION, NO INDECISION
BULL MARUBOZUOpen = Low, Close = HighBuyers in complete control
BEAR MARUBOZUOpen = High, Close = LowSellers in complete control
BULL CLOSING MARUSmall lower wick onlyBuyers dominated close
BEAR CLOSING MARUSmall upper wick onlySellers dominated close
Real Example — BankNifty Bear Marubozu · June 4, 2024 Election Results Day
June 4, 2024 — Indian election results. BankNifty opened at 49,265 on fears of coalition government and closed at 46,272 — a fall of nearly 3,000 points in a single session with almost no wicks. Nifty opened at 22,527 and closed at 21,884. These were textbook Bear Marubozus — sellers were in complete control from open to close with no meaningful buying attempt. The pattern flagged continuation: both indices fell further in the next 3 sessions before stabilising.

Trading with Marubozu

✓
Breakout confirmation

A Bull Marubozu breaking above a key resistance level is a high-conviction breakout signal. Volume should be above average to confirm institutional participation.

✓
Trend continuation

A Marubozu in the direction of the existing trend is a continuation signal, not reversal. It tells you the trend is accelerating — a good place to add to existing positions.

✗
Don't chase after a gap + Marubozu

When a Marubozu forms with a large gap, chasing the move is risky — the big move may already be priced in. Wait for a retest of the Marubozu body or a consolidation setup before entering.

Engulfing Patterns

The Engulfing Pattern is a two-candle reversal signal and one of the most reliable patterns in all of technical analysis. The second candle completely engulfs (covers) the entire body of the first candle — showing a complete takeover by the opposing side.

Bullish Engulfing

After a downtrend: a small bearish (red) candle is followed by a larger bullish (green) candle whose body completely covers the prior candle's body. This shows that buyers absorbed all the prior day's selling and then some — a strong reversal signal.

BULLISH ENGULFING vs BEARISH ENGULFING
BULLISH ENGULFINGGreen body engulfs red bodyAfter downtrend = strong buy
BEARISH ENGULFINGRed body engulfs green bodyAfter uptrend = strong sell

The Three Rules for a Valid Engulfing

1
The prior trend must exist

Bullish Engulfing must come after a downtrend (at least 3–5 falling candles or a significant support level). No trend = no reversal.

2
Body must fully engulf the prior body

The second candle's body must open below (for bullish) or above (for bearish) the first candle's body AND close beyond it. Wick overlap doesn't count — only bodies.

3
Larger body = stronger signal

The bigger the second candle relative to the first, the stronger the reversal. An engulfing candle that is 3× the size of the prior candle is more powerful than one that is barely larger.

Nifty Example — Bullish Engulfing at 20 EMA · Oct 2023
Nifty fell from 19,850 to 18,837 over 12 sessions. On Oct 26, after touching the 200-day EMA at 18,837, a Bullish Engulfing formed: prior day close 18,876 (red candle body 80 pts), engulfing candle open 18,820 and close 19,046 (green body 226 pts — nearly 3× the prior candle). Entry at the close of the engulfing candle at 19,046, stop below the pattern low at 18,837. Nifty reached 20,192 in the following 3 weeks.
7

Harami — Inside Reversal

The Harami (Japanese for "pregnant") is the opposite concept to Engulfing. Instead of the second candle being larger, it is smaller and contained within the first candle's body. After a large directional move, the market suddenly contracts — a sign that momentum is running out and indecision has entered.

HARAMI PATTERNS — SMALLER CANDLE INSIDE THE PRIOR CANDLE'S BODY
BULLISH HARAMISmall green inside large redAfter downtrend = reversal warning
BEARISH HARAMISmall red inside large greenAfter uptrend = reversal warning

The Harami is a warning, not a confirmed reversal signal. It must be followed by a confirming candle in the reversal direction. It's weaker than the Engulfing — but it's faster to form and can catch a turn earlier, giving a better risk/reward entry.

Harami Cross — Maximum Indecision

When the second candle in a Harami is specifically a Doji (body = near zero), it becomes a Harami Cross — the strongest version of the Harami. The Doji inside the prior candle's body represents maximum uncertainty following a large directional move. In Nifty, a Harami Cross at the 20 EMA or CPR level after 3–5 falling candles is a high-probability reversal setup.

⚠️

Harami trade management: Because the Harami is a warning signal (not confirmed reversal), position size should be half of your normal size on the entry candle. Add the other half only after the confirming close above the Harami high (bullish) or below the Harami low (bearish).

8

Morning Star & Evening Star

Three-candle patterns represent multi-session agreement on a reversal. The Morning Star (bullish) and Evening Star (bearish) are the most important three-candle patterns. They are highly reliable because three consecutive sessions all point the same way.

Morning Star Structure

1
First candle: Large Bearish

A large red candle continuing the existing downtrend. Sellers appear to be in full control. This confirms the bearish context that sets up the reversal.

2
Second candle: Small body (the "star")

A small body candle — can be green, red, or a Doji — that gaps down from the first candle. This is the indecision/transition candle. The gap shows sellers initially still in control, but the small body shows momentum stalling.

3
Third candle: Large Bullish

A large green candle that closes at least halfway into the first (red) candle's body. This is the confirmation — buyers have now taken over completely. The deeper into the first candle, the stronger the reversal.

Nifty Example — Morning Star at 200 EMA · March 2023
Day 1 (Mar 20): Nifty down from 17,250 to close at 16,988. Large red candle — sellers dominant. The 200-day EMA was at approximately 17,010.

Day 2 (Mar 21): Opened at 16,972, traded between 16,828 and 17,043, closed at 17,014. Small Doji-like candle — complete indecision. The 200 EMA acted as a floor.

Day 3 (Mar 22): Opened gap-up at 17,055 and closed at 17,359. Large green candle that penetrated deeply into Day 1's red candle body. Morning Star confirmed. Entry at the open of Day 3 (17,055), stop below the Day 2 low (16,828). Nifty ran to 18,888 over the next 6 weeks.

Evening Star — The Bearish Version

The Evening Star is the exact mirror: large green candle → small star candle (gaps up) → large red candle closing at least halfway into the first candle. It signals the end of an uptrend. The most powerful Evening Stars form at resistance levels, ATH zones, or where the gap-up on Day 2 fills with a gap-down on Day 3.

⚠️

On Indian intraday charts: True gaps are less common within a session. On 5-min or 15-min Nifty charts, a "gap" translates to a fast move between candles. The key element is the star candle's small body after a large directional candle — the gap is a "nice to have," not a hard requirement in intraday application.

9

Context is Everything

By now you've learned over a dozen patterns. Here is the single most important lesson that separates profitable pattern traders from losing ones: the pattern is 30% of the trade — the context is 70%.

The Three Context Filters

1
Level context — Is the pattern at a key price level?

Key levels: CPR (TC/BC/PP), prior day high/low, weekly open, 20/50/200 EMA, VWAP, ORB high/low, round numbers (22,000 / 23,000 etc). A Hammer at a random spot in the middle of range = low probability. A Hammer exactly at CPR Bottom Pivot = high probability.

2
Trend context — Is the pattern in the right direction vs the trend?

Trading reversals against a strong trend has a low success rate. Bullish patterns in a strong downtrend are pullback entries — risky. Bullish patterns in an uptrend after a pullback to support are continuation entries — higher probability.

3
Volume context — Did volume expand or contract?

A Bullish Engulfing on 3× average volume is far more powerful than the same pattern on below-average volume. Volume is the fuel behind the pattern's validity. Check the volume bar at the bottom of your chart for every pattern you identify.

Pattern + Level Confluence Examples

PatternLevelDirectionReliability
Bullish Engulfing200 EMA + CPR Bottom PivotLongVery High
HammerPrior day low + VWAP retestLongHigh
Evening StarAll-time high / key resistanceShortVery High
DojiMiddle of range, no key levelAnyLow
Shooting StarORB high after breakout failureShortModerate-High
HaramiAny level, no confirmationAnyLow
🧠

The 3-confluence rule: Before taking any pattern-based trade, identify at least 3 independent factors supporting your trade. Example: Bullish Engulfing (1) + at 20 EMA (2) + above-average volume (3). Three confluences = execute. One or two = pass or reduce size significantly.

Common Mistakes & Key Takeaways

🔍
Trading Patterns in Isolation
Identifying a Hammer and immediately buying without checking for context. Most standalone patterns fail more than they succeed without level confluence.
Fix: Always ask "is this at a key level?" before entering any pattern-based trade.
🔄
Confusing Reversal vs Continuation
A Hammer in the middle of an uptrend after just 2 pullback candles is not a reversal signal — it is a continuation signal (dip buy). Calling everything "reversal" leads to misreads.
Fix: First label the trend, then label the pattern's role within that trend.
⚡
Entering Before Confirmation
Buying the moment a Harami or Doji forms without waiting for the confirming candle. These patterns are warnings — not entry signals by themselves.
Fix: For single-candle patterns at support, enter at close. For Harami/Doji, wait for the next candle's direction first.
📉
Ignoring Volume
An Engulfing pattern on below-average volume is a low-conviction signal. Without volume expansion, there is no institutional participation behind the move.
Fix: Add a volume bar to your chart and require at least 1.5× average volume for two-candle and three-candle reversal patterns.
🕐
Applying Daily Patterns to 1-min Charts
Candlestick patterns lose reliability on very short timeframes (1-min, 2-min) due to random noise. Patterns on these charts have near-random outcomes.
Fix: Use 5-min or 15-min charts for intraday pattern trading in Nifty/BankNifty. Daily charts for positional trades.
📚
Memorising Too Many Patterns
Beginners try to memorise 30+ patterns and end up paralysed or seeing patterns everywhere. Most experienced traders use 5–6 patterns consistently.
Fix: Master these 6 first: Hammer, Shooting Star, Engulfing (both), Doji, Morning Star. Everything else is a variation.
⚡ Key Takeaways — Chapter 2
✓Candlestick patterns work because they capture buyer-seller psychology at specific price levels — not because of magic
✓Context (level, trend, volume) determines a pattern's probability — the pattern shape alone is only 30% of the trade
✓The Hammer and Shooting Star are the same shape — location (bottom vs top of trend) is what makes them bullish or bearish
✓Engulfing patterns are more reliable than single-candle patterns because they require two-session confirmation
✓Morning Star and Evening Star are the highest-reliability reversal patterns — three sessions of agreement is hard to fake
✓The Doji represents indecision: Gravestone Doji = bearish at tops, Dragonfly Doji = bullish at bottoms
✓Apply the 3-confluence rule: identify 3 independent factors before executing any pattern-based trade
✓Master 6 patterns well rather than knowing 30 poorly — Hammer, Shooting Star, Bullish Engulfing, Bearish Engulfing, Doji, Morning Star
