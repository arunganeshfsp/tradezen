Support & Resistance

Support and resistance are the invisible walls that price bounces between. Every trading strategy — whether based on candles, indicators, or options — comes back to these levels. Learn to identify them precisely, understand why they work, and use them in real Nifty trade setups.

What Are Support & Resistance?

Support is a price level where demand exceeds supply — buyers step in and prevent price from falling further. Resistance is a level where supply exceeds demand — sellers overwhelm buyers and prevent price from rising further.

These levels exist because of collective memory. When Nifty bounces from 22,000 three times over two months, a large number of traders notice this. On the fourth visit, even more buyers step in expecting the same result — which actually creates the bounce they were anticipating. This is how technical levels become self-reinforcing.

📌

Core principle: Support and resistance levels are not lines where price always bounces. They are zones of increased probability. Price tests them, sometimes breaches them slightly, then reverses. Your job is to trade the probabilities, not predict exact bounces.

Why These Levels Form

1
Prior highs and lows — memory of pain and profit

Traders who bought at a prior high and saw price fall will sell when price returns to their entry (to "get out even"). This selling creates resistance at prior highs. Conversely, traders who missed a rally will buy when price returns to a prior support level.

2
High-volume nodes — price spent time here

Areas where a lot of volume transacted are "accepted" price zones. When price leaves and returns, it tends to gravitate back to these nodes — creating support/resistance.

3
Institutional order placement

Large funds place iceberg orders at key levels. These orders absorb market orders and create the "invisible wall" effect. Retail traders see the result on their charts without knowing the cause.

Nifty — The 22,000 Level · 2024
The 22,000 level on Nifty acted as resistance twice in Jan 2024 (Dec 2023 high), then became support after the breakout. When Nifty pulled back to 22,000–22,050 in Feb 2024, it bounced strongly. This is textbook role reversal (covered in Section 4). Institutional buyers placed buy orders at 22,000 expecting support at the prior breakout level — and they were right. The level held and Nifty rallied to 22,526.
2

How to Draw Levels Correctly

Most beginners draw too many lines and create a chart that looks like a web — useless for actual trading. The goal is to identify the 3–5 most significant levels on any timeframe and watch those.

The Correct Drawing Method

1
Use wicks, not just candle bodies

Draw your level through the wick extremes, not through the body. Wicks represent the actual price where transactions occurred — they are real prints that were rejected. A level at the wick high is more meaningful than one through the candle body.

2
Count touches — more touches = stronger level

A level that has been tested 3 or more times is confirmed strong. Each test without a breakout shows the opposing force (buyers at support, sellers at resistance) is holding. Two touches = tentative level. Three or more = high-conviction zone.

3
Look left — older levels still matter

Levels formed months or even years ago still work. Nifty's 2022 low at 15,183 acted as a reference level for over 18 months. Before trading any level, scroll left to see if it was previously significant.

4
Use the weekly chart for the most important levels

Weekly chart levels carry more weight than daily, which carry more than hourly. When identifying the key S/R levels for a week of trading, start on the weekly chart and work down to daily, then to the intraday timeframe you trade.

⚠️

The "3 lines max" rule for intraday: When you open a 15-min Nifty chart for the trading day, draw no more than 3 horizontal lines above current price (resistance) and 3 below (support). More than that creates decision paralysis and constant second-guessing.

3

Static vs Dynamic Support/Resistance

Support and resistance comes in two forms: static (horizontal lines that don't move) and dynamic (lines that move with price, typically moving averages or trendlines).

TypeExamplesCharacteristicWhen to Use
StaticPrior high/low, round numbers, PDH/PDL, CPRHorizontal, exact price levelRange-bound markets, key inflection points
Dynamic20 EMA, 50 EMA, 200 EMA, VWAP, TrendlineMoves with price over timeTrending markets, pullback entries

Dynamic Support: EMAs in Nifty

In a bull trend, Nifty tends to find support at the 20 EMA on the daily chart during shallow pullbacks. During deeper corrections, it often finds support at the 50 EMA. The 200 EMA is the last line of defense — a close below it on the weekly chart has historically preceded major bear phases.

Nifty — 20 EMA as Dynamic Support · Aug-Sept 2023
During Nifty's bull run from July to Sept 2023, the 20-day EMA rose from approximately 19,200 to 19,800. Every time Nifty dipped toward the 20 EMA (19,250 on Aug 18, 19,430 on Aug 24), buyers stepped in within 1–2 sessions. Pullback traders used the 20 EMA touch as an entry trigger: buy at the close of the first candle to touch the 20 EMA, stop below it by 50–80 points, target the prior high.

Trendlines as Dynamic Support

A trendline connects two or more significant lows (uptrend) or highs (downtrend). Once it is tested and holds a third time, it becomes high-probability support/resistance. The key is to draw the line through the wicks of the most significant swing points, not trying to make it "fit" as many touches as possible.

4

Role Reversal — Support Becomes Resistance

This is one of the most powerful and reliable concepts in technical analysis: when a support level is broken, it often flips to become resistance on the next test from below. The opposite is equally true — a broken resistance becomes support on retest.

Why does this happen? Traders who were long at the support level and saw it break are now sitting on losses. When price rallies back to their entry, they sell to "get out flat" — creating the new resistance. This collective behavior is highly predictable.

Nifty Role Reversal — The 21,000 Level · Oct–Dec 2023
21,000 was major resistance for Nifty from Sept to Oct 2023. After a strong breakout on Nov 3 (closed at 21,117), Nifty pulled back. On Nov 14, Nifty tested 21,000 from above — the exact prior resistance level. It bounced from 20,971, confirming the role reversal: former resistance became support. Traders who identified this in advance had a near-perfect low-risk long entry with a tight stop below 20,971.

Strength of Role Reversal

The strength of the role reversal depends on two factors:

1
How long the original level held

A level that held for 3 months as resistance creates a stronger role reversal when broken than one that held for just 2 weeks. More traders have memory of the level = stronger psychology.

2
How fast/strong the breakout was

A large-volume, wide-range breakout candle creates a cleaner role reversal than a slow, grinding break. The explosive move traps more sellers in losing positions who will cover on the next test.

Round Numbers & Psychological Levels

Markets are moved by humans, and humans think in round numbers. In Nifty, the levels 22,000 / 23,000 / 24,000 / 25,000 are not just arbitrary numbers — they are massive psychological barriers where option writers, institutional traders, and retail traders all place orders simultaneously.

Options data amplifies round numbers further. Nifty options have maximum open interest at round strikes (22,000 CE / 22,000 PE). When Nifty approaches these levels, option writers defend their positions aggressively, which literally creates the support/resistance effect through order flow.

⚡

Intraday tip: Half-round numbers (22,500 / 23,500) are also significant — less powerful than whole thousands but still meaningful. On a 15-min chart, look for option chain max pain and strikes with the largest OI — these often act as intraday magnet levels.

6

Prior Day High/Low (PDH/PDL)

The Prior Day High (PDH) and Prior Day Low (PDL) are among the most reliable intraday support/resistance levels. Every professional intraday trader marks these before the session opens. Here's why they work:

When Nifty broke above a prior day high, all the short sellers from the previous session are now in a loss. As price retests the PDH from above, their anxiety peaks and many cover — adding to buying demand. When price breaks below a PDL, longs from the prior session are trapped.

BUY
Long above PDH retest

If today's Nifty opens above yesterday's high and then pulls back to retest the PDH level (as support), that is a high-probability long entry. Entry at the PDH level, stop below PDH by 30–50 points.

SELL
Short below PDL retest

If today opens below yesterday's low and retests the PDL from below (as resistance), that is a high-probability short setup. Entry at PDL, stop above PDL by 30–50 points.

Nifty PDH Retest — Jan 17, 2024
Prior day (Jan 16) high was 21,964. On Jan 17, Nifty opened gap-up at 22,002 (above PDH). By 10:15 AM, it had pulled back to 21,958 — testing the prior PDH from above. That was the entry for a long trade. Stop: below 21,920 (40 pts below PDH). Target: 22,200 (prior swing high). Nifty moved to 22,097 by 12:30 PM, a gain of approximately 130 points from entry.

Weekly and Monthly Levels

Higher timeframe levels carry more weight than lower timeframe ones. A weekly high or monthly open that aligns with a daily support level creates what traders call confluence — multiple reasons to expect a reaction at the same price zone.

Level TypeTimeframe WeightKey Levels to Watch
Monthly Open/High/Low⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ HighestFirst session of month open, monthly ATH, monthly range extremes
Weekly Open/High/Low⭐⭐⭐⭐ Very HighMonday open, prior week high/low
Daily PDH/PDL⭐⭐⭐ HighPrior day high, low, close
Intraday ORB⭐⭐ ModerateFirst 15-min or 1-hour high/low
Real-time CPR/VWAP⭐⭐ ModerateTC, BC, PP levels; VWAP deviation bands
✅

The confluence checklist: Before entering a trade near a S/R level, count how many independent sources agree. PDH + weekly high + round number + CPR top pivot = 4 confluences = very high probability resistance. One level alone = medium probability.

Nifty — Multi-Timeframe Confluence · Mar 2024
On March 28, 2024 (month-end), the following levels converged near 22,500:
• Monthly close (March) was 22,514
• Weekly high the prior week: 22,526
• CPR Top Camarilla was near 22,490
• Major round-number psychological resistance at 22,500

Four independent reasons to expect resistance at 22,500. Nifty was rejected sharply from this zone and retraced 8% over the following weeks. Traders who mapped these levels in advance knew to protect long positions and look for short setups near this zone.
8

Zones, Not Lines

Beginners think of S/R as exact price points. Professional traders think of them as zones — a range of 20–80 points on Nifty within which the market reacts. The exact bounce point can vary slightly from your drawn level without the level being "broken."

How Wide Should a Zone Be?

The zone width depends on the timeframe and volatility. A rough guide for Nifty:

TimeframeZone WidthExample
5-min intraday10–25 pointsLevel at 22,000 → Zone: 21,975 to 22,025
15-min intraday25–50 pointsLevel at 22,000 → Zone: 21,960 to 22,040
Daily chart50–100 pointsLevel at 22,000 → Zone: 21,950 to 22,050
Weekly chart100–200 pointsLevel at 22,000 → Zone: 21,900 to 22,100

When price enters a zone, slow down your analysis. Look for candle confirmation (Hammer, Engulfing, Doji) within the zone before entering. Don't enter at the first touch of the zone boundary — wait to see how price behaves inside it.

⚠️

Zone breach rule: A zone is "broken" only when price closes (on the timeframe you're trading) beyond the zone, not just touches it. A 5-minute candle closing 30 points below your support zone = break. A wick poking 10 points through = noise within the zone.

Building a Level Map for Nifty Trading

A level map is your pre-market preparation document — the list of key levels you expect price to interact with during the trading session. Building this before 9:15 AM each day keeps you objective during the chaos of live trading.

Pre-Market Level Mapping — Step by Step

1
Open a weekly Nifty chart — mark this week's range

Note the weekly open (Monday's opening price), prior week's high, prior week's low. These are the outer boundaries that define the current week's trading range.

2
Switch to daily — mark PDH, PDL, prior week levels

Note yesterday's high and low. Check if there are any daily candle extremes from 3–10 days ago that are near current price. These are your daily-level zones.

3
Add CPR levels from TradeZen

The CPR (Central Pivot Range) gives you the Top Pivot (TC), Bottom Pivot (BC), and Pivot Point (PP) for the day. These are calculated automatically from the prior day's OHLC — use the TradeZen Trade Flow tool to get today's values before 9:15 AM.

4
Note round numbers within ±500 points

Check which round-number thousands are within 500 points of current price (e.g., if Nifty is near 22,800, mark 22,500 and 23,000). These will act as intraday magnets.

5
Prioritise — choose your top 3 levels

From all levels identified, select the 3 most significant ones above and below current price. These are your primary trade zones for the day. Having a pre-decided list prevents "level shopping" during live trading.

Common Mistakes & Key Takeaways

📉
Drawing Too Many Lines
Adding 10–15 horizontal lines to the chart creates paralysis — every price action becomes "near a level." Too much clutter = no trade clarity.
Fix: Maximum 3 support + 3 resistance levels per trading session. Fewer, higher-quality levels beat many mediocre ones.
🎯
Expecting Exact Level Hits
Waiting for price to touch exactly 22,000.00 before acting. Price rarely hits a round number exactly — it bounces from a zone around it.
Fix: Define zones (±25–50 pts) before the session. Enter when price enters the zone and shows a pattern signal, not at the exact line.
🔄
Missing Role Reversals
Continuing to treat a broken support as support after it is clearly broken. This causes traders to "buy the dip" that turns into a crash.
Fix: After any level breaks with a strong close, immediately reclassify it as resistance (support→resistance) or support (resistance→support).
🔍
Ignoring Higher Timeframes
Drawing S/R only on a 5-min chart and missing the major weekly/monthly level that sits right at the same price — which is why the bounce was so strong.
Fix: Always map levels top-down: weekly → daily → hourly → 15-min. A level that appears on multiple timeframes is always more powerful.
📊
No Confluence Requirement
Trading every single S/R touch without requiring additional confirmation. This leads to many small losses from false bounces.
Fix: Require at least 2 confluences: e.g., S/R level + candlestick pattern, or S/R level + volume spike, before entering any trade.
📅
Not Updating Levels Daily
Using the same S/R levels from Monday all week without updating for PDH/PDL each day. Levels from 3 days ago are less reliable than yesterday's levels.
Fix: 5 minutes of pre-market level mapping every single session. The PDH/PDL change every day and should always be on your chart.
⚡ Key Takeaways — Chapter 3
✓S/R levels work due to collective trader memory — they are self-reinforcing because many people watch and act on the same levels
✓Draw through wick extremes, require 3+ touches for high-confidence levels, and always look left on the chart before marking a level
✓Role reversal is highly reliable: broken support becomes resistance, broken resistance becomes support on the first retest
✓Round numbers (22,000 / 23,000) are amplified by options data — max OI strikes are natural intraday magnets
✓PDH and PDL are the two most important intraday levels — mark them before 9:15 AM every day without exception
✓Think in zones, not lines: a level ±25–50 points is normal. Price poking through by a few points is not a break
✓Higher timeframe levels (weekly, monthly) carry more weight than lower ones — always map top-down
✓Build a pre-market level map every session: weekly range → PDH/PDL → CPR → round numbers → top 3 priority levels
