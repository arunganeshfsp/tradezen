Price Action Fundamentals

Price action is the foundation beneath every indicator, strategy, and trading system. Before you learn CPR, VWAP, or RSI — you need to understand how to read the raw language of the market: the candlestick. This chapter builds that foundation from scratch, with real Nifty examples.

What is Price Action?

Price action is the study of how a financial instrument moves over time using only its raw price — no indicators, no overlays, no algorithms. Every candlestick you see on a chart is the recorded result of millions of buy and sell decisions made by everyone from retail traders in Chennai to hedge fund desks in New York. Reading price action means learning to decode what those decisions tell you collectively.

Here is the core truth about all of technical analysis: every indicator is a derivative of price. RSI is calculated from price. EMA is calculated from price. VWAP is calculated from price × volume. If you understand price itself, you understand the source from which every other tool is derived. A trader who reads price well will always have an edge over someone blindly following indicator crossovers.

This is especially important for Nifty 50 intraday trading. Nifty moves fast — a 100-point swing can happen in 3 minutes. Indicators that lag by even one candle can have you entering as the move is ending. Price action, by contrast, is happening right now. The candle forming in front of you is live information.

💡

A useful mental model: Think of the market as an auction. Buyers are bidding prices up; sellers are pushing prices down. A candlestick is a 5-minute auction result. The body shows who won that auction. The wicks show where the other side briefly took control and failed. When you read price action, you are reading the live outcome of the auction process.

Where price action fits in your trading system

Price action is not a standalone trading system — it is the foundation on which every system is built. You use price action to:

1
Confirm entries and exits

A VWAP level or CPR TC might be your signal zone, but a specific candle closing at that level is your precise entry trigger. Without price action reading, you enter blindly into zones rather than at high-probability moments within them.

2
Judge the quality of a signal

Not all RSI crossovers or EMA touches are equal. A bullish signal accompanied by a strong green Marubozu candle is far more reliable than the same signal alongside a tiny Doji. Price action tells you how much conviction is behind a signal.

3
Read market context without tools

When your broker's platform freezes mid-session (it happens), a trader who reads raw charts can still make informed decisions. Understanding price action gives you independence from any specific tool or setup.

Anatomy of a Candlestick

Every candlestick encodes exactly four pieces of information about the period it covers: the Open, High, Low, and Close. On a 5-minute chart, each candle represents one 5-minute interval of trading. On a daily chart, each candle represents one full trading day.

HIGH
↕ upper wick
CLOSE
↕ body
OPEN
↕ lower wick
LOW
HIGH

The highest price reached during this candle's period. Shown at the top of the upper wick. Price went here but couldn't sustain it.

CLOSE (Bullish candle)

The last traded price when the candle closed. For a green candle, Close is at the top of the body — buyers ended higher than they started.

OPEN

The first traded price when the candle opened. For a green candle, Open is at the bottom of the body.

LOW

The lowest price reached during this period. Shown at the bottom of the lower wick. Price tested this but buyers stepped in.

⚠️

Bullish vs Bearish candle — the body flip: On a green (bullish) candle, Close > Open, so Close is at the top of the body and Open is at the bottom. On a red (bearish) candle, Close < Open, so Open is at the top and Close is at the bottom. New traders often confuse this — the open and close positions swap depending on candle colour.

🇮🇳 Real Example — Nifty 5-Minute Candle
On a typical intraday session, a single 5-minute candle for Nifty might read:

Open: 23,140  ·  High: 23,168  ·  Low: 23,122  ·  Close: 23,157

This is a green candle. The body runs from 23,140 (open) to 23,157 (close) — a 17-point gain. The upper wick extends 11 points above close to 23,168 — sellers pushed price back from that high. The lower wick extends 18 points below open to 23,122 — buyers defended the low and drove price back up. Net result: buyers won this 5-minute period, but there was a brief selling battle at the top.

The Marubozu — Maximum Conviction

A Marubozu (Japanese for "close-cropped") is a candle with no wicks — or extremely tiny ones — meaning the body spans almost the entire candle range. The open was the extreme, and the close was the other extreme. No resistance whatsoever from the other side.

📈 Bullish Marubozu
✅ Open = Low, Close = High (or very close)
✅ Buyers controlled the entire candle
✅ Not a single candle where sellers could push back
✅ Strong continuation signal in an uptrend
✅ As a breakout candle above resistance — extremely reliable
📉 Bearish Marubozu
🔴 Open = High, Close = Low (or very close)
🔴 Sellers controlled the entire candle
🔴 Buyers had no meaningful response
🔴 Strong continuation signal in a downtrend
🔴 As a breakdown candle below support — extremely reliable
🇮🇳 Real Example — Bearish Marubozu on Nifty Daily Chart
On 4-Jun-2024 (election results day), Nifty opened at 22,527 and closed at 21,884 — a massive 643-point fall with barely any wicks. This was a near-perfect Bearish Marubozu on the daily chart. There were no meaningful buying attempts throughout the entire session. Sellers were in complete control from the opening bell to close. Traders who recognised this candle type on the daily chart the following morning stayed bearish and avoided the trap of buying "cheap" prices in the days that followed.

The Spinning Top — Indecision at Its Purest

A Spinning Top has a small body (either colour) with long wicks on both sides. It looks like a spinning top toy. It means: buyers pushed price up during the period, sellers pushed it back down, and neither side ended up significantly ahead of where they started. It is the visual equivalent of a draw.

A Spinning Top in isolation tells you very little. But a Spinning Top after a strong trending move is powerful — the trend is losing momentum. The side that was dominating has suddenly met equal resistance. Watch for direction in the next candle for confirmation.

The Wicks — Rejected Prices

If the body tells you who won, the wicks tell you where the other side fought back. Every wick is a price level that was reached, tested, and then rejected. This is the most underappreciated information in candlestick reading.

Upper Wick — Selling Rejection

An upper wick forms when price rose during the candle period but sellers pushed it back down before close. The longer the upper wick, the more aggressive the selling at that high. This is evidence of supply — sellers were ready and waiting at that price level.

When an upper wick forms precisely at a known resistance level — a prior swing high, a CPR TC, a round number — it is not coincidence. Sellers placed limit orders there in advance. The wick is their fingerprint. This is one of the highest-probability short setups in intraday trading.

🇮🇳 Real Example — Upper Wick Rejection at CPR TC
Consider a day where Nifty's CPR TC is calculated at 22,050. The 10:15 AM candle opens at 22,020, pushes up to 22,064 (14 points above TC), then closes back at 22,028. The result: a 36-point red candle with a significant upper wick reaching exactly to the TC zone. This is sellers defending the TC level. The 14-point wick above TC was a stop-hunt — price swept above to trigger buy stops, then reversed. Traders who saw this wick at TC had a high-probability short entry on the close of that candle.

Lower Wick — Buying Rejection (The Hammer Signal)

A lower wick forms when price fell during the candle but buyers aggressively stepped in and pushed it back up. The longer the lower wick relative to the body, the more conviction buyers showed at the low. When this occurs at a support level — a prior swing low, CPR BC, VWAP, a round number — it is called a Hammer and is one of the most reliable bullish reversal signals.

🎯

The wick trader's rule: A wick at a known level has meaning. A wick in the middle of nowhere is just noise. Always identify your key levels first (CPR, VWAP, S/R, round numbers) and then look for wicks at those levels. Never reverse the process — don't look for wicks first and then try to justify a level.

Wick Proportions Matter

A rule of thumb: a wick is significant when it is at least twice the size of the candle body. A candle with a 10-point body and a 25-point lower wick is a clear Hammer. A candle with a 40-point body and a 10-point lower wick is just a normal green candle — the buyers won easily and a small lower wick is expected noise.

Candle Size in Context

A 30-point candle on Nifty might be enormous on a quiet day or completely normal on a volatile day. Candle size is always relative to recent context. There is no universal definition of a "large" candle — you measure it against the preceding 5–10 candles.

Average True Range — Your Sizing Reference

The Average True Range (ATR) measures the average daily or intraday range over a period. For Nifty, on most normal trading days, the 5-minute ATR is between 20–35 points. A candle of 60+ points on a normal day is therefore unusually large and carries significant information. On a budget day or event day (RBI policy, global selloff), the ATR can double — a 60-point candle is then just average.

📊

Relative size rule: When a candle is significantly larger than the recent surrounding candles, it means the balance of power shifted decisively in one period. This often occurs at breakouts, reversals, or key news reactions. These "breakout candles" are the market's way of shouting — they deserve your full attention.

The "Mother Candle" Concept

A Mother Candle is an unusually large candle that is followed by several smaller candles that fit entirely within its high-low range. The market is digesting the big move. This consolidation inside the mother candle often precedes the next directional move — in the same direction as the mother candle if the trend is intact, or in the opposite direction if the mother candle was a reversal signal.

🇮🇳 Example — Mother Candle Consolidation on Nifty
On a typical trending morning, Nifty forms a large green candle at 9:30 AM: Open 23,200, High 23,285, Low 23,195, Close 23,278 — an 85-point range candle. The next three 5-minute candles all trade between 23,240 and 23,280. They fit inside the mother candle's range. This is healthy consolidation. The market is absorbing the breakout before resuming. When the 4th candle closes above 23,285 (the mother candle high), that is the continuation entry signal — buyers have digested the move and are resuming the push higher.

The Nifty Opening — The Most Critical Zone

Nothing in intraday trading carries more information — and more danger — than the Nifty opening session from 9:15 to 9:30 AM IST. This is where 15-hour pent-up supply and demand collides in 15 minutes. Understanding what happens at the open is essential because it shapes the entire day's price structure.

Nifty Intraday Session Risk Zones
⚠️ DANGER ZONE
9:15 – 9:30 AM
Institutional orders hit simultaneously. Price swings 50–150 pts in minutes. Traps are common. Avoid entering.
⚡ SETUP ZONE
9:30 – 10:15 AM
ORB forming. Direction becoming clearer. First high-probability trades appear. Trade carefully.
✅ PRIME ZONE
10:15 AM – 1:00 PM
Trend established. Institutions participating. Best quality setups. Ideal trading window.
⚡ LUNCH ZONE
1:00 – 2:00 PM
Volume drops. Random moves. Many algo systems pause. Reduce size.
🔔 CLOSING ZONE
2:00 – 3:30 PM
Institutional squaring. Options unwinding. Moves can be sharp. Exit before 3:15.

Why the First 15 Minutes Are Dangerous

1
Overnight gap creates immediate imbalance

If global markets moved overnight (SGX Nifty, US futures, Asian markets), Nifty opens with a gap. That gap triggers stop losses, margin calls, and panic — all within the first few candles. The price swings are real but often temporary.

2
Institutional order book hits simultaneously

Large funds place overnight orders that execute at 9:15 AM. When a ₹500 crore buy order hits in the first minute, it can move Nifty 40–60 points instantly. Retail traders cannot react fast enough and often chase the move at the worst price.

3
Opening traps are engineered

A common pattern: Nifty opens 80 points gap up, retail buyers rush in, Nifty immediately reverses 120 points — trapping every buyer from the open. This is called a "gap fill reversal." It happens multiple times every month. The first 15-minute candle frequently contains an engineered trap.

The Opening Range — Your Directional Compass

The Opening Range (ORB) is the high and low formed in the first 15–30 minutes. Once these levels are defined, they become the most important price action reference for the rest of the day. A breakout above the opening range high confirms bullish direction. A breakdown below the opening range low confirms bearish direction.

The TradeZen Trade Flow tool calculates the ORB automatically and shows you live when a breakout is occurring. The candle that closes beyond the ORB is your entry trigger — not the wick, not the touch, the close.

Reading a Sequence of Candles

Individual candles give you snapshots. But markets tell their story through sequences. Price action is a narrative — each candle is a sentence, and the sequence is the paragraph. Learning to read sequences separates good traders from those who only react to single candles.

The Uptrend Sequence — Higher Highs, Higher Lows

An uptrend is defined by price making Higher Highs (HH) and Higher Lows (HL). This sequence tells you buyers are consistently stepping in at higher prices and sellers are failing to push price back to the previous low. Every new higher low is a foothold for the next push up.

When the Uptrend Breaks

An uptrend is broken when price makes a Lower Low — it drops below the previous Higher Low. This is the first warning that the trend may be reversing. It does not confirm a downtrend yet (you need a lower high too), but it is a signal to tighten stops and reduce long exposure.

↑
Healthy uptrend: each pullback holds above the prior low

Price makes HH → pulls back → holds above previous HL → pushes to new HH. This is the "staircase" pattern. Each Higher Low is a potential entry for traders who missed the initial move.

→
Warning: pullback breaks previous Higher Low

Price pulls back and closes below the previous Higher Low. The staircase pattern is broken. This is not a reversal confirmation but it means tighten your stop and do not add to longs.

↓
Confirmed reversal: Lower High followed by Lower Low

After the Lower Low, price rallies but fails to make a new high — forming a Lower High. This is now a confirmed downtrend structure. Exit remaining longs, look for short entries on rallies.

🇮🇳 Real Context — Nifty Trend Identification in Practice
During a strong trending morning, watch for this sequence on the 5-minute chart: Nifty makes 23,180 (HH₁), pulls back to 23,140 (HL₁), pushes to 23,220 (HH₂), pulls back to 23,170 (HL₂). As long as each pullback holds above the previous HL, the trend is intact and every dip is a buy opportunity. The moment price closes below 23,140 (the previous HL₁), the structure is broken and you stop buying dips.

Price Action vs Indicators

This is one of the most debated topics in trading. The truth is not that one is better than the other — it's that they serve different purposes and work best together. Here's an honest comparison:

📊 Price Action
✅ Zero lag — you see the candle forming in real time
✅ Works on all timeframes and all instruments
✅ Gives you the raw truth of what buyers/sellers are doing
✅ Does not repaint or change retroactively
⚠️ Requires practice and subjectivity — two traders can read the same candle differently
⚠️ Can be overwhelming without a framework for key levels
📈 Indicators
✅ Objective — RSI 68.4 is RSI 68.4 for every trader
✅ Good at quantifying momentum, trend strength, volatility
✅ Useful for filtering entries within a price action signal
⚠️ All lag — they are calculated from past prices
⚠️ Can give conflicting signals across different settings
⚠️ Over-reliance creates "indicator soup" — paralysis by analysis

The Right Relationship

Think of it this way: price action tells you what is happening. Indicators help confirm how strong it is. A Hammer candle at CPR BC is your price action signal. RSI at 32 (near oversold) at the same moment is your indicator confirmation. Together, they give you higher conviction than either alone.

This course teaches both — and every chapter on indicators will specifically explain how to combine them with the price action foundation you're building here.

⚠️

The most common mistake: Traders who rely entirely on indicators often find that by the time an indicator signals "buy," price has already moved 40 points. They buy at the worst possible entry. Price action readers enter at the first candle signal. Indicators are best used as filters, not primary entry signals, on fast-moving instruments like Nifty.

Common Mistakes

These are the most frequent price action errors made by traders in their first 6–12 months. Recognising them in yourself is the first step to fixing them.

🔴
Trading wicks as entries without level context
Seeing a long lower wick anywhere and buying it — even when there's no nearby support level. Random wicks are just noise.
✅ Fix: Identify your key levels first. A wick only matters when it forms at a level you already identified.
🔴
Entering during the first 15 minutes
Chasing the opening move because it "looks so strong." Opening moves frequently reverse — this is where most retail losses accumulate.
✅ Fix: Never enter in the first 15 minutes. Wait for the ORB to form and a confirmed breakout direction.
🔴
Reading candles without timeframe context
A Hammer on a 1-minute chart at noon means almost nothing. The same pattern on a 15-minute chart at a major support is significant.
✅ Fix: Always check at least two timeframes. Use 15-min for trend bias, 5-min for entry timing.
🔴
Ignoring the direction of the candle's close
Entering on price reaching a level rather than waiting for the candle to close there. Wicks frequently touch levels and reverse — you need the close to confirm.
✅ Fix: Enter only on candle close, not on price touch. This single habit eliminates most false entries.
🔴
Treating every Doji as a reversal
A Doji signals indecision — not reversal. Indecision in a strong trend often leads to continuation, not reversal.
✅ Fix: Wait for the next candle after a Doji to tell you who won. Never counter-trade a strong trend on a Doji alone.
🔴
Forgetting the trend when reading individual candles
Finding bearish candles in an uptrend and shorting. Against a strong trend, bearish candles are just pullback candles — they often resolve back in trend direction.
✅ Fix: Always establish the current trend structure (HH/HL or LH/LL) before entering any trade. Trade with the trend, not against it, unless at a major reversal zone.

Key Takeaways

📋 Chapter 1 Summary
✓Every candlestick encodes OHLC data — Open, High, Low, Close. The body shows who won. The wicks show where the other side fought back.
✓A large body = conviction. A small body = indecision. A Marubozu (no wicks) = complete domination by one side.
✓Wicks are only meaningful at known key levels — support, resistance, CPR TC/BC, VWAP, round numbers. A wick in the middle of nowhere is noise.
✓Never trade the first 15 minutes (9:15–9:30 AM). Wait for the ORB to form. Enter on candle close, never on a wick touch.
✓Uptrend = Higher Highs + Higher Lows. Downtrend = Lower Highs + Lower Lows. The trend is broken when the sequence first fails.
✓Price action has zero lag — it shows what is happening now. Indicators confirm how strong it is. Use both, with price action as the primary signal.
✓Candle size is relative — always compare a candle to the surrounding 5–10 candles. A large candle relative to recent ones signals a power shift.
✓Always enter on candle close — not on a touch, not on a wick. The close confirms the verdict of that auction period.
